## Interviews & Conversations

### ['DISSERVICE TO SOCIETY': Nvidia CEO PUSHES BACK on AI 'doomers,' says tech creates jobs](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Raq6df2PKak) — Fox Business (19:28)
Part two of Maria Bartiromo's interview with Jensen Huang, taped the day after Nvidia's record quarter (85% revenue growth, 92% data center growth, $80B buyback). Huang's frame for the entire AI stack is a "five-layer cake" — energy, chips, infrastructure, models, applications — and his core policy ask is that the US lead at every layer rather than narrowly defending one.

On **US-China chips**, Huang's argument is striking for its bluntness: China doesn't need US chips because Huawei has had a "record year" and is now exporting its own stack to compete with American firms globally. Implicit message: export controls have already lost their leverage; the question is whether US AI companies can outcompete on the application layer.

On **national security**, Huang confirmed the Department of War now has access to Nvidia's open-source stack so it can be modified for military applications, and that "most of the world's radar systems and imaging systems" already contain Nvidia chips — including those used in the recent Operation Epic Fury rescue in Iran's mountainous terrain. This is the most explicit Huang has been about Nvidia's defense posture.

On **jobs**, Huang directly contested Eric Schmidt's "50% of young people's jobs will be gone" framing (the line that drew boos at a recent graduation speech) as "complete nonsense." His counter-frame: AI is the largest manufacturing build-out in US history — chip plants, packaging plants, data centers, "trillions of dollars" of reshored investment — and information workers will be augmented, not replaced. "You're not going to lose your job to AI. You're going to lose your job to someone who is an expert in using AI." He singled out software engineers specifically: still needed, "if not more," but shifting from typing to problem-solving.

On **competition**, Huang acknowledged AMD, Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, and Cerebras (post-IPO) as serious competitors — but emphasized Nvidia *gained share* over the past year in hyperscalers and model builders while creating a new "physical AI" segment for manufacturing and industrial that has no real competitor yet.
